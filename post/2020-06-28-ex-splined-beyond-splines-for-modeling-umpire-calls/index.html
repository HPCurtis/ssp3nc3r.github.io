Ex-splined: beyond splines for modeling umpire calls

What do we know about how baseball umpires call balls and strikes? A lot, actually. As those familiar with the game know, balls and strikes are determined by umpires applying the written rules of the game, which means they visually compare the location of a pitched ball to both home plate and batter anatomy.

We’ll get to those rules below.

But published models of umpire calls ignore the rules and how umpires apply them. Instead, those models rely on fitting observational data to non-parametric functions like splines. The approach induces bias; the results do not reflect actual umpire calls. And decisions made from those models will not be optimal. Better approaches — grounded in the applied rules of the game, physics, geometry, and probability — exist.

First, we review applicable baseball rules and umpire training. Then, we explore some observational data of umpire calls from the 2019 season. Third, we consider approaches in the literature for modeling calls and demonstrate their bias. Fifth, we lay out a way to model calls using the rules.

Umpires are trained to apply the rules of baseball

The rules

Beginning with the rules, then, umpires call a pitch a strike when it “is not struck at, if any part of the ball passes through any part of the strike zone.” And:

The STRIKE ZONE is that area over home plate the upper limit of which is a horizontal line at the midpoint between the top of the shoulders and the top of the uniform pants, and the lower level is a line at the hollow beneath the kneecap. The Strike Zone shall be determined from the batter’s stance as the batter is prepared to swing at a pitched ball.

Figure 1: The hollow beneath kneecap, waist, and shoulders, shown in this MLB rules diagram, have been measured on similar adult males, soldiers.

[@MLB:2017ua]. Since home plate is a “17-inch square…” and a baseball has a 1.47-inch radius, the total rulebook width of the strike zone is about 19.9 inches. (Ibid., Rule 2.02).

Umpire training

Umpires necessarily judge the location of a pitch in relation to the strike zone from a less-than-ideal perspective. As the catcher blocks any view from directly behind the plate inline with the center of the strike zone, umpires are trained to judge baseball location viewed from the inside, top edge of the batter’s strike zone, described as the slot [@weberTheySeeEm2014]. From that view, pitches up or inside are relatively certain to be a ball. But the physics of that perspective lead to increased uncertainty in baseball location as its placement moves toward the low, outside corner. This positioning also creates asymmetry in strike probability by baseball location, depending on batter handedness.

Earlier work has primarily relied upon generalized additive models and splines, as summarised in [@spencerModelingUmpireCalls2018].

Review of published models

Those earlier models have included [@Deshpande:2017uc], [@Judge:2018wg], and [@Blum:2017uf]. More specifically, Deshpande and co-authors described a mixed effects model with an undisclosed generalized additive model as a smoothing function on pitched baseball location at the front edge of home plate. Their model, then, added effects for count, umpire, and catcher. As they explain, it’s a “hierarchical model to estimate each umpire’s probability of calling a strike, adjusting for pitch participants, pitch location, and contextual information like the count.” Blum and Judge include point estimates of probabilities based on pitch location from another model as data for theirs. Unlike those models, Spencer explicitly modeled pitch location in a joint model for modeling umpire calls with batters’ choices to swing, but still relied upon multivariate splines as a proxy for the underlying process [@spencerJointlyModelingChoice2018].

Bias when applied to observed umpire calls

Splines and related functions (gaussian processes) are advantageous when the forms of relationships or processes are not known. But as we’ve discussed, here we do have information as of yet unused suggesting the functional forms of the matchup. As such, gaussian process functions are comparatively too flexible, leading to overfitting, because they ignore readily available information. A spline function, for example, would allow the probability of a called strike to increase the farther the baseball location was from the center of the strike zone.

To see biase in practice, let’s re-consider the pitch locations called by, say, umpire ___. But here we will completely remove the variation and uncertainty in his calls. Instead, we re-code all pitches within the rule book strike zone as strikes, and those outside as balls.
